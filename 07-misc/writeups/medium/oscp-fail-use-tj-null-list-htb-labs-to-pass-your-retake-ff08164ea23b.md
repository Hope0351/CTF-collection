# OSCP Fail? Use TJ Null List & HTB Labs to Pass Your Retake

*A practical roadmap — from that red “FAIL” screen to an OSCP pass — leveraging TJ Null boxes, HTB Academy, and smarter lab time.*

I wish my *Offensive Security certification* journey began with a victory photo. Instead, it opened with a glaring red “FAIL” on the OSCP portal. That sting, however, rewired how I tackle big goals. If you’re staring at your own failed attempt — or just fear one — stick around. The lessons below turned my retake into a pass, and they can shave weeks off your own OSCP preparation.

## Why My OSCP Story Starts With FAIL

I’m Brazilian, and my journey into cybersecurity began close to home.
I enrolled in DCPT, a national pentest-prep course that armed me with the fundamentals: nmap scans, buffer overflows, the thrill of the first reverse shell. Later, a postgraduate program in Cyber Warfare gave me theory and discipline.

With that modest toolkit I go to the OSCP standalone exam: three months of lab access and one shot at the test. OSCP+ demands you compromise several live machines and deliver a professional report — no multiple-choice safety net, and the certification itself expires in three years, forcing you to keep learning.

I thought determination would fill any gaps. Spoiler: it didn’t.

## Four Mistakes That Sent Me Straight to the Retake

## 1. Hiding Inside the PDF

I clung to the official course material, all 800+ English-only pages. It was my first tech course not written in Portuguese, so I spent a full month just translating and note-taking instead of hacking. “Try Harder” quickly became “Read Slower.”

## 2. Neglecting the Labs

Because I burned so much time with the manual, I barely touched the lab network. Practical muscle memory — pivoting, post-exploitation, report writing — was exactly what I lacked on exam day.

## 3. Ignoring the TJ Null OSCP Prep List

There’s a legendary spreadsheet mapping the most OSCP-like machines on Hack The Box and OffSec Proving Grounds. I discovered it late, completed only 18 boxes, and walked into the exam under-rehearsed.

## 4. Skipping HTB Academy

When something in the OffSec PDF felt abstract, HTB Academy would have explained it with animated diagrams and hands-on checkpoints. Instead, I wrestled alone with syntax errors and frustration.

## Rebuilding the Strategy After Failure

- Finished every box on the TJ Null list (minus the “Challenging Yourself” tier).

- Patched gaps with HTB Academy modules on AD, report writing, and local exploits.

- Drafted a polished report template; no scrambling at 3 a.m.

- Simulated 24-hour marathons in Pro Lab Zephyr to stress-test endurance.

*OSCP SuccessI remember the first mock run-through*: my recon flow felt like muscle memory instead of guesswork — a night-and-day contrast to attempt #1

## The Second Attempt: 24 Hours, No Panic

When that timer restarted, the difference was immediate:

- Recon tools (`nmap`, `burp suite`, `linPEAS`) sang in rhythm.

- Familiar privesc paths jumped out — LDAP misconfigs, sudo abuse, kernel exploits.

- Reporting was plug-and-play; screenshots dropped into Markdown faster than caffeine into my bloodstream.

After nearly the full 24-hour window — yes, I squeezed in a few hours of sleep — the exam was still brutal: a sprawling attack surface with vulnerabilities lurking everywhere. But when I finally hit Submit and the result came back: I had passed.

## Lessons for Future Candidates

- Touch the keyboard early and often. Reading won’t replace shell time.

- Translate only what you must. Don’t let language anxiety steal your lab hours.

- Complete the TJ Null list — it’s free, community-curated, and eerily close to the real thing.

- Supplement with HTB Academy when a concept refuses to stick.

- Test yourself in a realistic network — Pro Labs Dante and Zephyr are my current go-to recommendation.
