# Understand Cryptography: The Best Way


*Photo by [Rachel Claire](https://www.pexels.com/@rachel-claire?utm_content=attributionCopyText&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=pexels) from [Pexels](https://www.pexels.com/photo/woman-standing-near-old-stone-wall-with-carved-images-4577718/?utm_content=attributionCopyText&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=pexels)*


This is a guide on how to understand the basics of cryptography by following online resources and a book. This path took me around 20 days with an average study of 5–6 hours per day.


Cryptography, whose first use dates back to 1900 BC ancient Egypt, is now one of the most widely used concepts in the world, thanks to the Internet and mobile communication.


While you are reading this, the air around you is full of messages flowing here and there and most of these messages are encrypted. In fact, the last website you visited, used encryption, the last ATM transaction you made, used encryption, the last email you sent, used encryption, the last WhatsApp message you sent, used encryption, and even the last phone call you made used encryption before sending your digitized voice.


As a tech enthusiast, you must be curious about how these algorithms work and how cryptography has evolved through centuries. Yes centuries, because after the Egyptians, Julius Caesar also used encryption for communicating with his army. He used Caesar cipher, also called as shift cipher. And then, in World War II, the Enigma machine which used symmetric encryption played an important role.


By following this path, you will learn the basics of both, symmetric and asymmetric key cryptography along with some commonly used protocols like Digital signatures, Message Authentication Codes(MACs), SSL/TLS certificates, and Hash functions which are based on cryptographic basics.

## How to start this adventure?


[Professor Christof Paar](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=w81afLAAAAAJ&hl=en) of Ruhr University has uploaded 25 video lectures on cryptography on his YouTube Channel “[Introduction to Cryptography by Christof Paar](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC1usFRN4LCMcfIV7UjHNuQg/videos)”. Each lecture is of around 90 minutes and contains detailed information about the subject topics.


These lectures are based on the book named “Understanding Cryptography: A Textbook for Students and Practitioners” which is 390 pages long and written by Prof. Christof Paar and [Prof. January Pelzl](https://de.linkedin.com/in/jan-pelzl-3677634). You can view sample chapters of this book on the website [crypto-textbook.com](https://www.crypto-textbook.com/), and you can buy it from [Amazon US](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/3642041000?ie=UTF8&tag=crypttextb-20&linkCode=as2&camp=1789&creative=9325&creativeASIN=3642041000) or [Amazon In](https://www.amazon.in/Understanding-Cryptography-Textbook-Students-Practitioners-ebook/dp/B014P9I39Q).


There are a lot of other books on this subject, and I have bought 2 of them, but I strongly advise you to get this book if you really want to understand the subject in the best way. This book has a very simple language and includes detailed information about events that gave rise to all these cryptographic algorithms. It has some historical stories which set some background before understanding the algorithm.


You will find that the videos and the book date back to 2010, but cryptography has not changed much in this one decade and almost all of the course is still relevant. He has also updated the chapter on the SHA-3 hash function in 2017.

## Ok, how to study?


Start from the first lecture, watch it completely and if a subtopic is not clear, then rewind and watch that part again. 90 minutes is a long time, so watch the videos at 1.75x or 2.x speed if you are comfortable.


After completing the lecture, open the book and read the topic. The Lectures are based on the book, and you will understand most of the concepts in the first read itself.


Now, again start the lecture from the beginning and keep the book with you. Start watching the video and follow the topic in the book. After completing a subtopic, start making notes from the book. Notes are helpful for revision especially if you have your exam or interview.


This might look like a long and tedious way, but if you are dedicated, you will complete at least one lecture in one day with notes and a complete understanding of the topic.


The encryption algorithms are easy, only the maths part needs more time. Cryptography involves a lot of maths, I was worried about it before starting this course. I watched the videos again and again, to get familiar with the maths concepts. It’s not as hard as I thought.


While going through this course, you will find it hard to understand certain topics. In that case, you should watch videos on some other YouTube channels. You can also read articles on that topic so that you will get familiar with the topic.

Fun facts about Cryptography:


- Theoretically, any encryption can be broken. Practically, to break encryption, we need strong computational power, and by “strong” I mean very very strong computational power. Today’s encryption cannot be broken even by using all supercomputers in the world.

- If Quantum computers become a reality in the future, then by using strong algorithms they might be able to break most of today's encryption. This means that all the encrypted messages that we send today can be broken in the future. Provided, the messages are stored and accessible at that time. This is bad for all government's secret messages.

- The first widely used encryption algorithm was the “Data Encryption Standard” developed by IBM and published in 1975. It was later modified by the NSA in 1976.

- Today, Advanced Encryption Standard(AES) is the most widely used encryption for encrypting messages. The original name of AES was “Rijndael” and was designed by two Belgian Cryptographers.

- When you open a website, the padlock (🔒) icon in the address bar of the browser implies that the browser has verified the website’s identity using its SSL/TLS certificate and that all the data you sent and received will be encrypted.

- Whatsapp uses the Signal Protocol for providing end-to-end encryption. This protocol was developed by the Signal Technology Foundation, which also developed the Signal messaging app.
This protocol uses asymmetric encryption for exchanging a secret key between the users. This key is then used to encrypt the messages using the AES symmetric encryption.
